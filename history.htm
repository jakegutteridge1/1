Timeline of Theobalds Palace, which later became Cedars Park

Historians - can you assist us?

23/7/1383 - The manor of Cullynges is owned by Mr William Attemore.

3/8/1383 - Being indebted to William de Tongge by 101 pounds, Attemore hands Cullynges to him, along with another estate called Le Mores. Cullynges is renamed Tongs. A study on the property mentions 76 acres of crops, 91 of pasture, 10 of meadow, and 5 1/2 of wood, as well as a grand main building.

1441 - Master of St Anthony's Hospital John Carpenter, his son and Chancellor of the Exchequer John Somerset acquire the property and rename it Thebaudes, although other spellings were common before the final, Theobalds, became the standard, at an unknown date.

1563 - Sir William Cecil purchases the Theobalds estate from a London mercer named John Elyott. Almost immediately, he begins building a small mansion on the the estate for his son Thomas.

27/7/1564 - Queen Elizabeth enjoys her first recorded visit to Theobalds, and plans another.

1564 - In preparation for the Queen's next visit, Cecil heavily expands the property to better accommodate her (despite having bought the estate with the intention of keeping it as a quiet family home) - he begins building a grand house, completed in 1585. By the end of the 1560s, Theobalds was England's largest private house.

22/9/1571 - Elizabeth pays a second visit to Theobalds. Cecil gifts her a drawing of the house.

1572 - A visit by the Queen is recorded.

1572 - Cecil acquires the manor of Perieres, and uses it to enlarge Theobalds.

5-6/1575 - The Queen stays at the Palace for two weeks, during which time Elizabeth's Privy Council holds eight meetings.

1577 - A visit by the Queen is recorded.

7/1591 - The Queen visits Theobalds and is presented with the Hermit's Welcome. She stays around 10 days.

1592 - A visit by Jacob Rathgeb (Secretary to the Duke of Wirtemberg, Germany) is recorded. Frederick of Moempelgard (next in line for the position of Duke of Wirtemberg) visited several English cities and towns in 1592 - it is unclear if Rathgeb was accompanied by Frederick on his trip to Theobalds.

1593 - The Queen enjoys a 9-day visit, which cost Sir William Cecil some 2500 pounds.

The Queen begins to stay the Palace for long periods of time. During these stays, lavish performances, sporting events and other entertainments are held, all at Cecil's expense.

1594 - A visit by the Queen is recorded.

1596 - A visit by the Queen is recorded.

1598 - Sir William Cecil dies, his son Robert inherits Theobalds.

1602 - Frederic Gerschow, Secretary to the Duke of Stettin Pomerania visits the Palace and notes 52 painted & carved trees in the Green Gallery, each representing an English province, displaying Arms of Earls and Barons. There were also decorations depicting produce grown in these regions.

24/3/1603 - Elizabeth dies of sepsis.

3/5/1603 - King James I makes his first arrival at Theobalds on a journey from Edinburgh to London. He stays there for 4 days and is paid respect by local Lords and appoints several new members of the English and Scottish nobility. Many extravagant entertainments take place. The arrival is described as follows:

There came before him some of the nobility, some barons, knights, esquires, gentlemen and others. Among them were the Sherrill of Essex and most of his men, the trumpets sounding next before his Highness...his Majesty not riding continuously betwixt the same two noblemen, but sometimes with one and sometimes with another as seemed best to his Highness; the whole nobility of the land around him observing no place of nobility, but all bare headed; all of whom alighted at the first courts door, save only his Majesty who rode along still, with four noblemen laying hands upon his steed. In this manner he came to the court's door where I myself stood, where he alighted, from his horse, from which he had not gone ten princely paces when there was delivered to him a petition by a young gentleman, his Majesty returning his gracious answer that he should be heard and have justice. At the entrance to that court stood several noblemen among whom was Robert Cecil, who, there meeting his Majesty, conducted him into his house - all which was practiced with so great applause of the people as could be - hearty prayers and throwing up of hats.

5/1605 - Fulke Greville carries out a survey of Theobalds and suggests enlarging some windows in one of the galleries.

1606 - Christian IV of Denmark accompanies James I (his brother-in-law) on his second visit to the Palace. The 5-day stay included heavy drinking and entertainments (known as The Hours), the final of which is described as disastrous in John Harington's account. The visit cost Sir Robert Cecil 1180 pounds, plus 284 for presents and 23 to Inigo Jones for the masque props.

5/1607 - Robert Cecil exchanges Theobalds for Hatfield House, officially giving the Palace to Queen Anne, although some texts state it was given to her husband King James, as he owned Hatfield House. A masque is performed to celebrate the exchange.

1607 - c. 1612 - Cecil and his gardener Munten Jennings work together to expand the property and its gardens. Cecil chooses to demolish and re-build some of the buildings.

7/1613 - The King's favourite dog, Jewel, is accidentally shot during a hunt. The King was furious and stormed exceedingly for a while before realising that it was his wife, Queen Anne, who shot the dog. As an apology for his anger, he gifted her a diamond worth 2000 pounds (although records suggest he only paid 1500).

1614 - James I of England and Christian IV of Denmark stay at the Palace together for a second time.

7/1615 - James invites Polish-Lithuanian nobleman Tomasz Zamoyski to hunt with him at Theobalds.

1/1618 - James suffers from gout, and a play is performed for him, which he reportedly does not enjoy.

9/1618 - James orders the demolition of two newly-built tobacco shops near Theobalds, as his Courtiers disliked them. Munten Jennings, the gardener at Theobalds, is instructed to build a silkworm house on the estate.

1620/21 - James has a wall built around the estate.

9/1/1622 - After dinner, the King rides to the New River to see the ice on the water. He falls in head first and is helped out by Sir Richard Young, who returns him to a warm bed at Theobalds.

27/3/1625 - After residing at the Palace for his final years, James draws his last breath following a string of severe illnesses and a stroke earlier in the year.
Charles I is crowned king at the gates of Theobalds, inheriting the estate, only staying there on occasion as he had complained in 1624 that there is no kind of field-hawking there.

30/1/1649 - Charles I dies, his son Charles II inherits Theobalds.

1650 - A parliamentary survey of Theobalds estimates its size as 2500+ acres, and determines that it is in excellent condition and not fit for demolition. The report is misread and a great portion of the Palace is taken down.

1651 - Charles II is exiled from England after being defeated by Oliver Cromwell at the Battle of Worcester.

1660 - Charles returns from exile.

1661 - The estate is granted to George Monck, 1st Duke of Albemarle, who then passes it to his son, Christopher.

1687 - Christopher dies and the property is returned to the Royals.

1689 - King William III grants the Palace to Hans William Bentick, 1st Earl of Portland.

between 1699 and 1774 - The manor of Perieres is sold, becoming part of Beaumond Hall. It was apparently held jointly with Periers, so Periers must have been sold as well.

23/11/1709 - William Earl of Porland dies, his sons inherit the property.

1762 - William Cavendish-Bentick, 3rd Duke of Portland sells Theobalds to George Prescott.

from 1763 - Prescott builds a mansion on the grounds, which became known as Theobalds Park.

1765-1770 - Prescott builds a number of brick houses on the grounds, and a flint folly.

1783 - A survey of the property says The former Palace of Theobalds [is] now in a ruinous condition.

late 1700s - Theobalds is passed to Oliver Cromwell (namesake of his famous grandfather). The Prescott family must have continued to live here at this time, as they apparently left Theobalds Park around 1820, and they seemingly took back ownership.
(We would appreciate some clarity from any local historians with knowledge on this matter.)

c. 1820 - The Prescott family leave Theobalds Park and begin renting it to the Meux family.

1860 - The Meux family purchase the property.

1841 - Henry Meux (1st Baronet) dies, leaving Theobalds Park to his son, Henry Meux (2nd Baronet), who makes great changes to the estate.

1883 - Henry Meux (2nd Baronet) dies and his son Henry Bruce Meux (3rd Baronet) inherits the estate.

1885 - Henry Bruce Meux and his wife Lady Meux move into Theobalds Park.

1886 - Some extensions are made to Theobalds Park.

1887 - A water tower is built at Theobalds Park.

1880 - London's Temple Bar catches the eye of Lady Meux after its dismantlement in 1887, and it is purchased from the City of London and transported from Farringdon Road to Theobalds, and reconstructed at a cost of 10 thousand pounds. Lady Meux often entertained guests, including Winston Churchill, in the upper chamber.
In the same year, a new kitchen, gun room, swimming pool and indoor roller-skating rink are added to the estate.

1910 - Lady Meux dies and Hedworth Lambton inherits Theobalds, changing his surname to Meux as per her request.

1919 - Sir Hedworth Meux signs a Covenant that bestows part of the Theobalds grounds to the people of Cheshunt to be used as a public park, called (the) Cedars.

Now (the) Cedars Park

2/7/1921 - Sir Hedworth Meux and the Earl of Cavan officially open the park to the public.

1921 - Female Mark IV Tank 2740 from the Great War is moved into Cedars Park with a special street ceremony.

WWII - Some western parts of the Theobalds grounds, including a building, are used by the Royal Artillery.

Date unknown - The grounds are (briefly) used as a riding school for the Metropolitan Police.

1940 - The Cedars Park Tank is sold for scrap to Messrs Cox & Danks for 27 pounds, 16 shillings and 10 pence, to support the Second World War effort.

1950s - Land on Theobalds Lane formerly used for farming (believed to specialise in tomatoes and cucumbers) is used as a base for several new homes, which are now on the lane and its adjoining roads.

1951-1969 - Theobalds Park House, the only building that remains intact today, serves as Theobalds Park Secondary School.

1969-early 1980s - The house serves as an adult education centre.

1990s - The house is refurbished for use as a conference hall.

Date unknown - The house becomes a hotel.

2001-2004 - The Temple Bar arch is returned to the City of London and dismantled & reconstructed near St Paul's Cathedral, after being left to decay in Theobalds Park despite being a Scheduled Ancient Monument.

2002 - New entrance gates are installed at the front of Cedars Park to commemorate the Golden Jubilee of Queen Elizabeth II. They include the numbers '1952' and '2002', and modern artwork of historical figures and events at Theobalds.

20/3/2007 - Broxbourne Council receives 50,000 pounds from the Heritage Lottery, to support development of Cedars Park required for the first stage of a Heritage Lottery grant application.

13/7/2008 - The Caribana Steel Band performs in Cedars Park.

9/2008 - Broxbourne Council applies for the next part of the Heritage Lottery grant.

4/2009 - Broxbourne Council receives 173,000 pounds from the Heritage Lottery to develop Cedars Park prior to the final stage of the grant application.

9/2010 - A Cultural Festival is held at Cedars Park.

2011 - Broxbourne Council receives a Heritage Lottery grant of 1.89 million pounds to improve and conserve Cedars Park. Several new features were added to the park shortly after. (This information needs verification as this value may refer to the combined funds including previous grants)

1/6/2014 - A "Big Lunch" event is hosted by the Society Of Caribbeans & Africans, in conjuction with Halifax.

24-25/8/2014 - The English Civil War Society re-enacts the Civil War at Cedars Park. There are also arts & crafts, and re-creations of life at that time, such as outfits and spreads of period food.

12/7/2015 - ChExS Grow Club 'Veg Fest' is held.

2016 - Broxbourne Council receives lottery funding, which is used to revamp some of the features around Cedars Park, including the pond.

4/6/2017 - Local charity "Crest of a Wave" holds a music festival for children, known as Cedarsfest, in Cedars Park.

2018 - The grass on the Venusberg mound is replaced with artificial turf.

1/7/2019 - A replica tank based on 2325 - a Male Mark IV - is placed in Cedars Park, on the original tank plinth.

14/7/2019 - Steelpan festival "Pan In The Park" is held at Cedars Park.

7/11/2019 - A Tommy Statue, modelled to represent a Great War soldier, is placed in Cedars Park.

11/11/2019 - The first Armistice Day remembrance service is held at Cedars Park.

22/12/2019 - A Christmas Carol Service is held in Cedars Park.

[No Armistice Day service in 2020 due to Covid concerns]

11/2021 - A container is constructed in the old car park, whose purpose will be publicly announced in due course.

11/11/2021 - The second Armistice Day remembrance service is held at Cedars Park.

19/12/2021 - A Christmas Carol Service is held in Cedars Park.

Today, many structures that stood at Theobalds are still standing in Cedars Park, registered as Scheduled Ancient Monuments.
