Timeline of Theobalds Palace and Cedars Park
(Also historically spelled Tybolles, Tibolls, Tibbles, Theballs, Thebaudes, Thibaulds)

We have tried our best to ensure all information is correct, however due to the many years between now and when most of these events happened, we cannot guarantee that all accounts happened exactly as described. Please also note that not all events were recorded, and it is very likely that Queen Elizabeth I visited Theobalds on other occasions that are not mentioned here. Dates are in Day/Month/Year format. Additions and corrections are welcomed at jakant@mail.com.

23/7/1383
The manor of Cullynges is owned by Mr William Attemore.

3/8/1383
Being indebted to William de Tongge by 101 pounds, Attemore hands Cullynges to him, along with another estate called Le Mores. Cullynges is renamed Tongs. A study on the property mentions 76 acres of crops, 91 of pasture, 10 of meadow, and 5 1/2 of wood, as well as a grand main building.

1441
Master of St Anthony's Hospital John Carpenter, his son and Chancellor of the Exchequer John Somerset acquire the property and rename it Thebaudes, although other spellings were common before the final, Theobalds, became the standard, at an unknown date.

1563
Sir William Cecil purchases the Theobalds estate from a London mercer named John Elyott. Almost immediately, he begins building a small mansion on the the estate for his son Thomas.

27/7/1564
Queen Elizabeth enjoys her first recorded visit to Theobalds, and plans another.

1564
In preparation for the Queen's next visit, Cecil heavily expands the property to better accommodate her (despite having bought the estate with the intention of keeping it as a quiet family home) - he begins building a grand house, completed in 1585. By the end of the 1560s, Theobalds was England's largest private house.

22/9/1571
Elizabeth pays a second visit to Theobalds. Cecil gifts her a drawing of the house.

1572
A visit by the Queen is recorded.

1572
Cecil acquires the manor of Perieres, which is used to enlarge Theobalds.

2-3/1573
The Queen stays at the Palace for two weeks.

1574
A Painted (also known as Green) Gallery is completed.

5-6/1575
The Queen stays at the Palace for two weeks. Eight Privy Council meetings were held.

1577
A visit by the Queen is recorded.

1583
Elizabeth visits Theobalds accompanied by the Earls of Leicester and Warwick, the Lord Admiral, Lord Howard, Lord Hunsdon, Sir Christopher Hatton and Sir Francis Walsingham. They stay there for four days. Elizabeth meets Edward de Vere at the Palace.

1587
A visit by the Queen is recorded.

7/1591
The Queen visits Theobalds and is presented with the Hermit's Welcome. She stayed around 10 days.

1592
A visit by Jacob Rathgeb (Secretary to the Duke of Wirtemberg, Germany) is recorded. Frederick of Moempelgard (next in line for the position of Duke of Wirtemberg) visited several English cities and towns in 1592 - it is unclear if Rathgeb was accompanied by Frederick on his trip to Theobalds.

1593
The Queen enjoys a 9-day visit, which cost Sir William Cecil some 2500 pounds.

The Queen begins to stay the Palace for long periods of time. During these stays, lavish performances, sporting events and other entertainments are held, all at Cecil's expense.

1594
A visit by the Queen is recorded.

1596
A visit by the Queen is recorded.

1598
Sir William Cecil dies, his son Robert inherits Theobalds.

1602
Frederic Gerschow, Secretary to the Duke of Stettin Pomerania visits the Palace and notes 52 painted & carved trees in the Green Gallery, each representing an English province, displaying Arms of Earls and Barons. There were also decorations depicting produce grown in these regions.

24/3/1603
Elizabeth dies of sepsis.

3/5/1603
King James I arrives at Theobalds from Scotland. He stays there for 4 days and is paid respect by local Lords and appoints several new members of the English and Scottish nobility. Many extravagant entertainments take place.

5/1605
Fulke Greville carries out a survey of Theobalds and suggests enlarging some windows in one of the galleries.

1606
Christian IV of Denmark accompanies James I (his brother-in-law) on his second visit to the Palace. The 5-day stay included heavy drinking and entertainments (known as The Hours), the final of which is described as disastrous in John Harington's account. The visit cost Sir Robert Cecil 1180 pounds, plus 284 for presents and 23 to Inigo Jones for the masque props.

May 1607
Robert Cecil exchanges Theobalds for Hatfield House, officially giving the Palace to Queen Anne, although some texts state it was given to her husband King James, as he owned Hatfield House. A masque is performed to celebrate the exchange.

1607 - c. 1612
Cecil and his gardener Munten Jennings work together to expand the property and its gardens. Cecil chooses to demolish and re-build some of the buildings.

7/1613
The King's favourite dog, Jewel, is accidentally shot during a hunt. The King was furious and 'stormed exceedingly for a while' before realising that it was his wife, Queen Anne, who shot the dog. As an apology for his anger, he gifted her a diamond worth 2000 pounds (although records suggest he only paid 1500).

1614
James I of England and Christian IV of Denmark stay at the Palace together for a second time.

7/1615
James invites Polish-Lithuanian nobleman Tomasz Zamoyski to hunt with him at Theobalds.

1/1618
While King James was suffering from gout, a play was performed for him, which he did not enjoy.

9/1618
James orders the demolition of two newly-built tobacco shops near Theobalds, as his Courtiers disliked them. Munten Jennings, the gardener at Theobalds, is instructed to build a silkworm house on the estate.

1620/21
James has a wall built around the estate.

9/1/1622
After dinner, the King rides to the New River to see the ice on the water. He falls in head first and is helped out by Sir Richard Young, who returns him to a warm bed at Theobalds.

27/3/1625
After residing at the Palace for his final years, James draws his last breath following a string of severe illnesses and a stroke earlier in the year.
Charles I is crowned king at the gates of Theobalds, inheriting the estate, only staying there on occasion as he had complained in 1624 that 'there is no kind of field-hawking there'.

30/1/1649
Charles I dies, his son Charles II inherits Theobalds.

1650
A parliamentary survey of Theobalds estimates its size as 2500+ acres, and determines that it is in excellent condition and not fit for demolition. The report is misread and a great portion of the Palace is taken down.

1651
Charles II is exiled from England after being defeated by Oliver Cromwell at the Battle of Worcester.

1660
Charles returns from exile. The estate is granted to George Monck, 1st Duke of Albemarle, who then passes it to his son, Christopher.

1687
Christopher dies and the property is returned to the Royals.

1689
King William III grants the Palace to Hans William Bentick, 1st Earl of Portland.

between 1699 and 1774
The manor of Perieres is sold, becoming part of Beaumond Hall. It was apparently 'held jointly with Periers', so Periers must have been sold as well.

23/11/1709
William Earl of Porland dies, his sons inherit the property.

1762
William Cavendish-Bentick, 3rd Duke of Portland sells Theobalds to George Prescott.

1765-68 or 70
Prescott builds a number of brick houses on the grounds, as well as a flint folly and two small grottoes.

1783
A survey of the property says 'The former Palace of Theobalds [is] now in a ruinous condition'

c. 1820
The Meux family begin renting the Theobalds estate and acquire it shortly after.

1888
Lady (Val) Meux purchases London's Temple Bar from the City of London. It is dismantled and transported from Farringdon Road to Theobalds, and reconstructed at a cost of 10 thousand pounds. Lady Meux often entertained guests in the upper chamber, including Winston Churchill.

1919
Sir Hedworth Meux signs a Covenant that bestows part of the Theobalds grounds to the people of Cheshunt to be used as a public park, called (the) Cedars.

2/7/1921
Sir Hedworth Meux and the Earl of Cavan (Rudolph Lambart?) officially open the park to the public.

1921
Female Mark IV Tank 2740 from the Great War is moved into Cedars Park with a special street ceremony.

WWII
Some western parts of the Theobalds grounds, including a building, are used by the Royal Artillery.

Date unknown
The grounds become a riding school for the Metropolitan Police.

1940
The Cedars Park Tank is sold for scrap to Messrs Cox & Danks for 27 pounds, 16 shillings and 10 pence, to support the Second World War effort.

1950s
Land on Theobalds Lane formerly used for farming (believed to specialise in tomatoes and cucumbers) is used as a base for several new homes, which are now on the lane and its adjoining roads.

1955
The remaining standing building becomes a secondary school.

1969
The building becomes an adult education centre.

1990s
The building is refurbished for use as a conference hall.

Date unknown
The building becomes a hotel.

2001 - 2004
The Temple Bar arch is returned to the City of London and dismantled & reconstructed near St Paul's Cathedral, after being left to decay in Theobalds Park despite being a Scheduled Ancient Monument.

2002
New entrance gates are installed at the front of Cedars Park to commemorate the Golden Jubilee of Queen Elizabeth II. They include the numbers '1952' and '2002', and modern artwork of historical figures and events at Theobalds.

20/3/2007
Broxbourne Council receives 50,000 pounds from the Heritage Lottery, to support development of Cedars Park required for the first stage of a Heritage Lottery grant application.

9/2008
Broxbourne Council applies for the next part of the Heritage Lottery grant.

4/2009
Broxbourne Council receives 173,000 pounds from the Heritage Lottery to develop Cedars Park prior to the final stage of the grant application.

9/2010
A Cultural Festival is held at Cedars Park.

2011
Broxbourne Council receives a Heritage Lottery grant of 1.89 million pounds to improve and conserve Cedars Park. Several new features were added to the park shortly after.

24-25/8/2014
The English Civil War Society re-enacts the Civil War at Cedars Park. There were also arts & crafts, and re-creations of life at that time, such as outfits and spreads of period food.

1/7/2019
A replica tank based on 2325 - a Male Mark IV - is placed in Cedars Park, on the original tank plinth.

7/2019
Steelpan festival "Pan In The Park" is held in Cedars Park.

7/11/2019
A Tommy Statue, modelled to represent a Great War soldier, is placed in Cedars Park.

11/11/2019
The first Armistice Day remembrance service is held at Cedars Park.

[No Armistice Day Service in 2020 due to Covid concerns]

11/2021
A container is constructed in the old car park, whose purpose will be publicly announced in due course. Added by Jake Gutteridge 17/11/2021

11/11/2021
The second Armistice Day remembrance service is held at Cedars Park.

Today, many structures that stood at Theobalds are still standing in Cedars Park, registered as Scheduled Ancient Monuments.
