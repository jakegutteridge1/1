Welcome to Cedars Park

Cedars Park is a public park located on Theobalds Lane in Cheshunt, Hertfordshire. The grounds once held Theobalds Palace, which was a significant royal estate that accommodated several royals in its heyday. This was during a time when England was an absolute monarchy, meaning that the country was run from these very grounds. Therefore, the park is of great historical and national interest.

In 1919, Admiral Sir Hedworth Meux donated the grounds to the people of Cheshunt as a public park, protecting it with a covenant, and the area was opened to the public on 2 July 1921. Over the years, the grounds have been expanded, and they now cover around 19 acres. Today, despite busy dual carriageways on two sides, the park retains a tranquil atmosphere, and many ruins of Theobalds Palace and subsequent structures survive, now serving as charming focal points and a reminder of the Elizabethan, Jacobean and Georgian architectural styles. Many of these are Scheduled Ancient Monuments.

Cedars Park is managed by Broxbourne Borough Council, with the Friends Group volunteers providing assistance in maintenance and organising public events. Cheshunt Extended Services also frequently hold activities in the park to support the local community. The council has received funds amounting to some 1.9 million from the Heritage Lottery Fund over recent years, which has been used to add several new features to promote the rich history. The park has received a Green Flag Award every year since 2009.

The park is fully wheelchair-accessible, and on-site facilities include toilets, a cafe, and an animal centre.

This community website was independently launched in 2021 (others existed in the past) and is managed by volunteers.

Early Spring Greetings
11 February 2022

Abundant crocuses and snowdrops recently arrived in their usual annual recurrence, bringing colour to the parkland, which is much-welcomed due to the currently bare trees. Grounds Maintenance also heavily cut some trees and shrubbery, which has greatly tidied up the park's appearance.

  • Flint Arch - A folly and two huts built in 1765 survive in today's park.
  • Maze - Replica based on what is believed to have been the design of a maze at Theobalds.
  • Venusberg - Raised spiral path, replicating a garden viewing mound at Theobalds.
  • Duck Pond - Theobalds had many extravagant water features, and a single pond remains today.
  • Birds - Just a few of the many bird species that live at Cedars Park.
  • Wooden Carvings - Reflecting on some animals that were kept at Theobalds.
  • 17th Century Disability Gardens - Sensory flowerbeds for blind and disabled people.
  • Celebration Gates - New entrance gates added in 2002 to celebrate Queen Elizabeth II's Golden Jubilee.
  • Bee Boles - The wall of the "Great Garden" at Theobalds held a number of bee boles, which remain today.
  • Theobalds Square - A small complex of homes constructed in the 1760s, which do not remain intact today except for some outbuildings, which serve as the cafe, toilets, and meeting room.
  • Ghost Stories - The park community has shared some interest accounts of what they believe to be ghosts in the park over the years.
  • Relations with the U.S. - Officials from Waltham, Massachusetts have donated several cork trees to Waltham Cross since the 1930s, which were planted in Cedars Park.
