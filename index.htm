Welcome to Cedars Park

Cedars Park is a public park located on Theobalds Lane in Cheshunt, Hertfordshire. Once the Royal Palace of Theobalds, which was regularly visited by Queen Elizabeth I and later became King James I's favourite residence, the grounds (approximately 19 acres in size) are of significant historical and national interest. England was an absolute monarchy during James I's reign, meaning that for much of the time between 1607 and 1625 - when James I lived here - the country was run right from these grounds. The palace was largely destroyed during the English Civil War, as many of the rooves contained valuable lead, which was sold to support the troops. After acquisition by the Meux brewing family, Admiral Sir Hedworth Meux signed a covenant in 1919 that bestowed the grounds as a public park for the people of Cheshunt to enjoy, which was officially opened on 2 July 1921, named "The Cedars".

Over 100 years after its opening, despite busy dual carriageways on two sides, the park retains a tranquil atmosphere, and many ruins of Theobalds Palace and subsequent structures survive, now serving as charming focal points, as well as a reminder of the Elizabethan, Jacobean and Georgian architectural styles.

Cedars Park is managed by Broxbourne Borough Council, with the Friends Group volunteers providing assistance in maintenance and organising public events. The council has received funds amounting to some 1.9 million from the Heritage Lottery Fund over recent years, which has been used to add several new features to promote the rich history. The park has received a Green Flag Award every year since 2009.

This independent community website was launched in 2021 (others existed in the past) and is managed by volunteers independent of the Council or Friends Group.

Early Spring Greetings to the Cedars Park Community
11 February 2022

Abundant crocuses and snowdrops recently arrived in their usual annual recurrence, bringing colour to the parkland, which is much-welcomed due to the currently bare trees. Grounds Maintenance also heavily cut some trees and shrubbery, which has greatly tidied up the park's appearance.

  • Arboretum - Cedars Park has a sizeable and mature arboretum with a diverse array of species.
  • Photo Gallery - Community photographs of the park, from the present day right back to the 1920s.
  • History - As the site of the former Theobalds Palace, Cedars Park has an incredibly rich and significant history.
  • Key People in History - These grounds have accommodated many important people over the years.
  • Memorials - A listing of the memorials (mostly trees and benches) that have been planted in the park to remember a loved one.
  • Dogs - Cedars Park has a close-knit community of regular dogs and walkers.
  • Covenant - 1919 document of Admiral Meux's donation of the park's grounds to the people of Cheshunt.
  • Great War Centennial Corner - Memorial comprising a replica tank, Tommy statue, and centennial bench to Remember the Fallen in the Great War.
  • Flint Arch - A folly and two huts built in 1765 survive in today's park.
  • Maze - Replica based on what is believed to have been the design of a maze at Theobalds.
  • Venusberg - Raised spiral path, replicating a garden viewing mound at Theobalds.
  • Duck Pond - Theobalds had many extravagant water features, and a single pond remains today.
  • Wooden Carvings - Reflecting on some animals that were kept at Theobalds.
  • 17th Century Disability Gardens - Sensory flowerbeds for blind and disabled people.
  • Celebration Gates - New entrance gates added in 2002 to celebrate Queen Elizabeth II's Golden Jubilee.
  • Bee Boles - The wall of the "Great Garden" at Theobalds held a number of bee boles, which remain today.
  • Theobalds Square - A small complex of homes constructed in the 1760s, which do not remain intact today except for some outbuildings, which serve as the cafe, toilets, and meeting room.
  • Ghost Stories - The park community has shared some interest accounts of what they believe to be ghosts in the park over the years.
  • Relations with the U.S. - Officials from Waltham, Massachusetts have donated several cork trees to Waltham Cross since the 1930s, which were planted in Cedars Park.
